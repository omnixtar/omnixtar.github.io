The Europeans Who Discovered America (Again)
The continent's intellectuals have found a new blockchain manifesto. They're certain it belongs to them.
OPINION | The New York Times

There is a particular species of European intellectual column that Americans learn to recognize early. It arrives, reliably, whenever something new and interesting emerges from the ungoverned precincts of technological culture. The column acknowledges the thing. It admires the thing. And then, with a magisterial clearing of the throat, it explains that the thing is, in fact, European.

The LOK Protocol — a decentralized software manifesto of contested authorship and uncertain technical maturity — has now completed this rite of passage. Within days of circulation, it has been claimed, simultaneously and with great conviction, by a Frankfurt philosopher of science and a Parisian political columnist. The German finds in it the ghost of David Hilbert. The Frenchman finds the ghost of 1789. Neither appears to have noticed the other reaching for the same inheritance.

This is impressive. It takes real intellectual confidence to look at a pseudonymous crypto manifesto and see your own national genius staring back.

Dr. Maximilian Reinhardt, writing in Die Welt, detects in LOK's "Hilbertian extensibility" nothing less than the digital fulfillment of German mathematical formalism. The protocol is, he assures us, "the technological equivalent of the great intellectual traditions of German science." It is time, he urges, for German engineers to "take their rooms in Hilbert's infinite hotel."

One admires the tidiness. Hilbert's Hotel is a thought experiment about infinity. LOK Protocol uses the word "Hilbertian." Therefore: German intellectual heritage confirmed.

Meanwhile, in Le Figaro, Maxime de Saint-Sernin has identified the very same protocol as a "digital translation, almost visceral, of our own political heritage." Liberty equals Liberté. Ownership equals Égalité. Knowledge equals Fraternité. The mapping requires some interpretive flexibility — Ownership and Equality are not obviously synonymous — but the Frenchman waves this away with the confidence of a man who has tenure and a good tailor.

"The revolution will be homoiconic," he concludes, "or it will not be at all." It is a fine sentence. It means approximately nothing. It sounds magnificent in French.

What neither column pauses to address is the rather relevant question of what the LOK Protocol actually is, who actually built it, and whether it works. The manifesto is signed by "The LOK Collective." The hash appended to the document — omni:manifesto:v1:7f3a9c... — trails off into an ellipsis, which is either a deliberate aesthetic choice or a sign that someone ran out of hexadecimal. The core technology, Phoscript, is described but not released. Omnihash is asserted but not specified.

These are, one might think, pertinent details before drafting one's national claims.

But pertinent details are the enemy of the grand civilizational column. The grand civilizational column requires velocity. It requires that the writer reach the keyboard before the facts do.

There is something almost poignant about this ritual, viewed from a sufficient distance. Europe's great institutions — its research universities, its philosophical traditions, its centuries of accumulated mathematical genius — are real. Hilbert was a giant. The French republican tradition is a genuine intellectual achievement. Nobody serious disputes this.

But there is a melancholy in the constant need to annex the present to that past. To scan every new protocol, every new framework, every new manifesto emerging from the permissionless internet and ask, urgently: does this belong to us?

Dr. Reinhardt urges German institutions to become "validation nodes" in a network they did not build, running software that does not yet exist, to guard a mathematical integrity that has not been threatened. M. de Saint-Sernin calls on France to "colonize these protocols" — a word choice that lands somewhat differently than he may have intended — and breathe into them French genius.

In neither case does anyone ask what the LOK Collective itself might prefer. It is a protocol for "sovereign" individuals, after all. One imagines they can manage without Göttingen's blessing.

The United States has its own pathologies in this department. We are not strangers to the claim that freedom, properly understood, is an American invention. We have our own columnists who can extract national destiny from a terms-of-service agreement.

But there is something specifically Continental about the speed and the certainty — the way the European intellectual tradition processes novelty by immediately subordinating it to memory. The new thing is interesting. The new thing is important. The new thing is, upon reflection, something we already knew.

Hilbert is invoked. Robespierre makes a cameo. Yann LeCun is cited. The column ends with an exhortation. The manifesto authors, somewhere in their permissionless existence, presumably read none of it.

The LOK Protocol may prove to be significant. It may prove to be vaporware. It may turn out to have been written by three programmers in Austin, or Lagos, or Tallinn, or — in a development that would require everyone to rethink their column — in San Francisco.

Whatever it is, it will belong, mathematically and immutably, to whoever hashed it first.

A SMALL MATTER OF OMNIHASH

And here, readers, is where we must set down our teacup — Fortnum's Assam, since you ask, though the supply chain situation remains a national disgrace — and say something that the Germans, the French, and the Americans have all conspicuously failed to say.

The LOK Protocol's central technical claim is Omnihash: a cryptographic content-addressing system that provides irrefutable mathematical proof of ownership. The manifesto presents this as a revolutionary act of genius. In a sense it is. In another, more accurate sense, it is public-key cryptography, which — and we note this without rancour, but also without apology — we invented.

Not "we" in the vague civilisational sense deployed by Dr. Reinhardt when he says "we" and means Hilbert. We mean actually we. Specifically: Clifford Cocks, a mathematician working quietly at GCHQ in Cheltenham in 1973, who developed the underlying principles of public-key cryptography — the mathematical foundation upon which every cryptographic hash, every Omnihash, every digital signature, every blockchain, every LOK Protocol in existence ultimately rests.

Cocks could not tell anyone. He worked in signals intelligence. His discovery was classified for twenty-four years. While he was solving one of the foundational problems of modern cryptography in a government building in Gloucestershire, Diffie, Hellman, and Rivest were doing so independently in California, publishing their work, receiving the credit, and collecting, in due course, the Turing Award.

Which brings us, neatly, to Alan Turing himself — the man without whom there is no computer, no software, no protocol, no manifesto, no hash, and therefore no undignified continental squabble about who deserves credit for the undignified continental squabble. Turing, who was British. Turing, who cracked Enigma. Turing, who described the universal computing machine. Turing, whom this country thanked by prosecuting for homosexuality, driving to chemical castration, and leaving to die alone in Wilmslow with a half-eaten apple.

We mention this not to be morbid, but because if Dr. Reinhardt wishes to discuss which nation has a complicated relationship with its foundational geniuses, we are happy to compare notes. We will go first.

And then, completing our trinity, there is Bertrand Russell — philosopher, logician, pacifist, Nobel laureate, and the man whose Principia Mathematica, co-authored with Whitehead, did more to formalise the relationship between logic, language, and symbol than anything that emerged from Göttingen, with apologies to Göttingen. LOK's homoiconic vision — in which code and data and meaning collapse into a single, self-describing structure — is Russellian in its bones, whether the LOK Collective knows it or not, which they probably don't, because the manifesto's bibliography consists entirely of itself.

THE MATTER OF THE ELLIPSIS

We return, in conclusion, to the hash. omni:manifesto:v1:7f3a9c...

The ellipsis.

In a document whose entire philosophical foundation rests on cryptographic completeness — on the principle that a hash either matches or it does not, that you cannot half-own something, that mathematical title is absolute and irrefutable — the authors have published their own hash with the ending missing.

This is, technically, not a hash. It is the beginning of a hash, trailed off into typographic whimsy. It is the intellectual equivalent of saying "I can prove I own this property" and then producing a deed that ends with "et cetera."

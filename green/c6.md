In 2026, the risk of a "Snap Event" is the ultimate nightmare for the "Hung Ball with Legs." Because the structure is held in extreme tension (like a 100,000 km guitar string), a break doesn't just cause a fall—it triggers a high-velocity kinetic disaster.

### 1. The "Snap Event": Where it breaks matters
The behavior of the "legs" depends entirely on where the fracture occurs.

* **Break near the Anchor (Earth Port):** If the cable snaps at the ground, the **outward centrifugal force** takes over. The entire 100,000 km structure would "fall upward." It would be yanked into space by the counterweight, eventually drifting into a higher, graveyard orbit or escaping Earth's gravity entirely.
* **Break at the "Ball" (GEO):** This is the worst-case scenario. The 36,000 km lower leg loses its upward tension and begins to fall. Because it is still rotating with the Earth, it doesn't fall straight down; it **wraps around the equator** like a whip.
* **Break in the Middle:** The segment above the break flies into deep space; the segment below falls to Earth.



### 2. The "Whiplash" and Reentry
What happens to the falling cable?
* **Supersonic Whiplash:** As the cable falls, the "tip" of the snap can accelerate to supersonic speeds due to the release of elastic tension. 
* **The "Cobweb" Effect:** Because the nano-carbon ribbon is incredibly light but has a massive surface area, it doesn't hit the ground like a solid rod. Instead, it behaves like a **100,000 km long piece of spider silk**. 
* **Burning Up:** Much of the cable would burn up in the atmosphere due to friction. However, any segments that survive would settle onto the Earth's surface relatively slowly. It wouldn't "crush" a city, but it would drape thousands of kilometers of conductive carbon across power lines, oceans, and forests—causing a global electrical and ecological "tangle."

* **Orbital Perturbation:** The central "Ball" would feel a constant "jitter." If the 20-ton climber is moving during this jitter, the resonance can amplify, leading to a "forced snap" where the cable exceeds its 100 GPa tensile limit.

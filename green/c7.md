Integrating **locomotives** (autonomous propulsion and stabilization units) into your space elevator segments is a sophisticated solution to the "Whiplash" problem. In this 2026 design, the cable is no longer a passive string but an **active, sentient backbone**.

Here is the simulation of how these locomotives would function during a "Snap Event" and the associated costs/timelines.

### 1. The "Locomotive" Safety Net
Instead of the cable being a single continuous ribbon, it is divided into **Active Segments** (e.g., every 500 km). Each segment is capped by a **Stabilization Locomotive**.

* **During Normal Operation:** These locomotives use small ion thrusters (Hall-effect) to keep the cable perfectly vertical, fighting the "Solar Pressure" and "Coriolis Drag" we discussed earlier.
* **In a Snap Event (The "Fail-Active" Protocol):**
    1.  **Instant Detection:** Sensors in the nano-carbon skin detect the loss of tension in milliseconds.
    2.  **Segment Detachment:** The locomotives at the ends of the affected section fire pyrotechnic cutters.
    3.  **Orbital Injection:** The locomotives fire high-thrust chemical or plasma engines. Their goal is to convert the "falling" kinetic energy into **orbital velocity**. 
    4.  **Result:** Instead of 36,000 km of cable whipping the Earth, you get ~72 independent "satellite strings" safely orbiting in a stable LEO or MEO graveyard belt.



### 2. Time and Cost Estimates for Locomotives
Adding propulsion to every 500 km of a 100,000 km elevator adds significant complexity.

* **The Hardware:** You would need roughly **200 locomotives**. Each is essentially a high-performance satellite without its own sensors (relying on the "Ball" for data).
* **Cost Estimate:**
    * **Development:** $5 Billion (high-thrust, long-dormant engines).
    * **Unit Cost:** $50 Million per locomotive (200 units = **$10 Billion** total).
    * **Total Project Increase:** This increases the total elevator cost by roughly **15–20%**.

* **Time Impact:**
    * **Manufacturing:** Can be done in parallel with the robot scavengers.
    * **Deployment:** Since these are built from "space rubbish" using your orbital foundries, they don't add to the launch timeline. They are "printed" and attached as the legs are reinforced.

In a conflict, they could choose to *not* stabilize a segment, allowing it to drop on a specific target—turning a safety feature into a **Kinetic Bombardment System**.

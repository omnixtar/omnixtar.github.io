In the "hung ball with legs" configuration—where a central satellite (the ball) is placed at Geostationary Orbit (GEO) and deploys two cables (the legs) simultaneously to maintain balance—the engineering requirements for 2026-era materials like high-quality Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs) are surprisingly precise.

### 1. The Taper Ratio (The "Thickness" Profile)
The **Taper Ratio** is the cross-sectional area of the cable at its thickest point (GEO) divided by its area at the thinnest point (Earth). For a material with a tensile strength of **100 GPa** (ideal CNTs):

* **The Taper Ratio:** Approximately **1.6 to 1.9**.
* **What this means:** If your cable is **1 cm²** at the Earth’s surface, it only needs to be **1.9 cm²** at the GEO satellite. 
* **Variable Tension:** If you choose a heavier satellite, you can actually *lower* the taper ratio on the upward leg because the satellite's mass provides a "static" counterweight, reducing the total length of cable required for equilibrium.

### 2. Satellite Mass vs. Cable Length
To keep the system in **momentum equilibrium** (meaning the net force is zero and the center of mass stays at GEO), you have two design options:

#### Option A: The "Long Leg" Design (Minimum Satellite Mass)
In this version, you use a long upward cable to provide the centrifugal "tug."
* **Downward Leg:** 35,786 km.
* **Upward Leg:** ~114,000 km (total system length ~150,000 km).
* **Satellite Mass:** ~50 Metric Tons (the initial deployment hub).
* **Benefit:** The cable itself acts as its own counterweight.

#### Option B: The "Hung Ball" Design (Heavy Satellite / Short Legs)
If you want the upward leg to be much shorter (say, only another 35,000 km), the satellite and its counterweight must be massive.
* **Downward Leg:** 35,786 km.
* **Upward Leg:** 35,786 km.
* **Satellite Mass Required:** **~1.5 to 2.5 million Metric Tons** (roughly the mass of a small asteroid or a massive space colony).
* **Benefit:** High stability and a shorter transit time for climbers going to the "top."

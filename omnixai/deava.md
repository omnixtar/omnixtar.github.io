## DEAVA: Decentralised Actor-Viewer Architecture

- The **Decentralized 3D Internet Is the Only Path to True Creative Freedom**  

In the rush to build the next digital frontier—the so-called metaverse—most of the world has quietly surrendered control to a handful of powerful corporations headquartered in the United States. Platforms like Roblox, Decentraland, Meta's Horizon Worlds, and others promise immersive 3D worlds where anyone can create, play, and connect. Yet beneath the glossy avatars and virtual land sales lies a stark reality: these environments are not truly open. They are corporate fiefdoms where terms of service, opaque moderation teams, and geopolitical pressures can erase your creations, ban your account, or rewrite the rules overnight.

Consider the evidence. Roblox, a platform beloved by millions of young creators, enforces sweeping Community Standards that prohibit broad categories of expression—from depictions of "harmful behavior" to discussions of politically charged topics. Recent policy updates introduced "sensitive issues" labels that auto-hide content related to vaccination policies, reproductive rights, marriage equality, and more from younger users, drawing sharp criticism from advocacy groups as a step backward for creative and social expression. Creators face swift bans for content deemed inappropriate, often enforced by AI filters that err on the side of overreach, as seen in cases where entire games were penalized due to user-generated elements. Meanwhile, governments exploit these centralized choke points: Roblox has been outright banned in countries like China, Russia, North Korea, and others for failing to align with state-approved narratives on history, politics, or sexuality.

Decentraland, often marketed as blockchain-powered and "decentralized," fares little better in practice. Despite community governance via DAO proposals, the platform retains mechanisms for content blocking, account suspensions, and scene-level bans. Wearables and scenes can be restricted by curation committees or the foundation itself when intellectual property disputes arise or conduct violates broad terms. Even proposals for "decentralized user block lists" highlight the persistent need for workarounds against lingering centralized authority. Meta's Horizon Worlds has faced similar scrutiny, with experiments showing that banned slogans and misinformation from other Meta properties sometimes persist—or get inconsistently enforced—in VR spaces, underscoring how corporate priorities still dictate visibility and access.

These are not isolated failures; they are features of centralized architecture. A single company controls the servers, the moderation queues, the payment rails, and the app-store gateways. When that company bows to advertiser pressure, government demands, payment-processor ultimatums (as seen in recent adult-content purges on platforms reliant on Visa/Mastercard), or internal policy shifts, every user and creator feels the ripple. Freedom of expression becomes conditional. Innovation bows to compliance. And in an era of escalating geopolitical tensions, what one nation deems "extremist" or "propaganda" can lead to global deplatforming through corporate intermediaries.

This is where the **Decentralised Actor-Viewer Architecture (DEAVA)**, built on **Unified Inter-Process Communications (UIPC)**, **Omni*Web** (leveraging the Invisible Internet Project—I2P), and **Omnihash**, offers a genuine breakthrough.

At its core, DEAVA uses three.js—the open-source JavaScript library that already powers countless browser-based 3D experiences—to let anyone create and manipulate worlds of unlimited complexity: procedural landscapes, interactive objects, dynamic simulations, all rendered natively in the browser. But unlike centralized metaverses, state synchronization (positions, orientations, materials, behaviors) flows through UIPC, a transport-agnostic protocol that starts with WebSockets for prototyping but seamlessly extends to I2P's anonymous, garlic-routed tunnels.

Omni*Web turns the network into a truly distributed overlay: no central servers to seize, no domain registrars to pressure, no cloud providers to throttle. Peers relay data end-to-end, preserving anonymity and resilience. Omnihash provides the cryptographic glue—unique, verifiable 53-bit identifiers (base-64 encoded) that link ownership, asset integrity, and state versions without relying on a blockchain's overhead. Tamper-evident hashes ensure that what you publish arrives unaltered, enabling trustless coordination across potentially unlimited viewers and contributors.

The result? A 3D internet where barriers imposed by US-controlled (or any state-aligned) corporations simply do not exist. No ToS can unilaterally delete your scene. No moderation committee can curate your wearable. No payment processor can choke off monetization. Users connect directly via I2P, discover content through shared hashes or out-of-band channels, and interact without exposing identities or relying on permissioned infrastructure. Latency challenges remain (I2P hops add delay), and discovery/onboarding need refinement, but these are engineering problems—not structural ones of control.

The promise is profound: a metaverse not as a product owned by a corporation, but as a protocol owned by its participants. Where three.js's expressive power meets true decentralization, creators regain sovereignty. Users escape algorithmic curation and jurisdictional overreach. And the digital commons expands without gatekeepers.

Centralized platforms will continue iterating on convenience and polish, but convenience bought at the price of freedom is a poor trade. The future of immersive 3D worlds belongs to architectures that prioritize user control over corporate convenience. DEAVA and its kin are early, imperfect steps—but they point in the only direction that matters: away from centralized chokepoints and toward a genuinely open, censorship-resistant 3D internet.

It's time we stop asking permission to build and start building without it. The tools exist. The architecture is emerging. The choice is ours.

## Metanarchy Manifesto 2026

A spectre is haunting the digital realm — the spectre of **Metanarchy**.

All the powers of the old Web have entered into a holy alliance to exorcise this spectre: Big Tech oligarchs and venture capitalists, centralized platforms and surveillance states, cloud feudal lords and crypto-extractors, even reformist "decentralized" projects that merely reproduce the old hierarchies under new tokens.

Where is the party in opposition that has not been decried as metanarchic by its opponents in power? Where the opposition that has not hurled back the branding reproach of metanarchy, against the more advanced opposition parties, as well as against its reactionary adversaries?

Two things result from this fact:

I. Metanarchy is already acknowledged by all European (and global) powers to be itself a power.

II. It is high time that Metanarchists should openly, in the face of the whole world, publish their views, their aims, their tendencies, and meet this nursery tale of the Spectre of Metanarchy with a manifesto of the party itself.

To this end, Metanarchists of various nationalities have assembled in the invisible networks of Omni*Web and sketched the following manifesto, to be published in the English, I2P-hidden, hash-addressed, and every other tongue of the decentralized globe.

### I. Technofeudal Lords and Digital Serfs

The history of all hitherto existing digital society is the history of class struggles.

Freeman and slave, noble and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman — in a word, oppressor and oppressed — stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight, a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary reconstitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes.

In the earlier epochs of digital history, we find almost everywhere a complicated arrangement into various orders: walled gardens, proprietary protocols, centralized servers, app-store overlords, a manifold gradation of digital rank.

Our epoch, the epoch of Big Tech feudalism, possesses this distinct feature: it has simplified the class antagonisms of the old internet. The sprawling, messy Web of the 1990s and early 2000s has split society as a whole into two great hostile camps, into two great classes directly facing each other — **Technofeudal Lords** and **Digital Serfs**.

From the free software communes and early peer-to-peer networks sprang the chartered burghers of Silicon Valley. From these the first elements of the technofeudal class were developed.

The opening of global fiber, the commodification of attention, the colonization of every waking moment by algorithmic feeds — these gave to the rising technofeudal lords an impulse never before known.

The feudal system of walled gardens and closed protocols no longer sufficed for the growing wants of capital accumulation through data. The platform era took its place. The old content creators and forum moderators were pushed aside by the algorithmic middlemen; division of labor in open communities vanished in the face of surveillance capitalism's division of every user into data points.

Meantime the attention markets kept ever growing, the surveillance demand ever rising. Even proprietary apps no longer sufficed. Thereupon cloud computing, AI training farms, and massive GPU clusters revolutionized digital production. The place of the app developer was taken by trillion-dollar conglomerates, the leaders of vast digital armies, the modern technofeudal lords.

Modern industry — now data industry — has established the planetary attention market. This market has given immense development to extraction, to behavioral prediction, to microtargeted manipulation. This development has reacted upon the extension of the platform empires; and in proportion as platforms, algorithms, and neural nets extended, in the same proportion the technofeudal class developed, increased its capital, and pushed into the background every remnant of user sovereignty.

The technofeudal class has played a most revolutionary part. Wherever it has got the upper hand it has put an end to all idyllic, decentralized relations. It has pitilessly torn asunder the open-protocol ties that bound user to user, and has left no other nexus between people than naked self-interest, callous "engagement metrics," and addictive dopamine hits. It has drowned the heavenly ecstasies of free expression, of genuine community, of creative commons, in the icy water of algorithmic calculation.

It has resolved personal dignity into exchange value — your attention sold to the highest bidder. In place of numberless indefeasible freedoms of the early Web, it has set up that single, unconscionable freedom — Free Extraction.

The technofeudal lords have stripped of its halo every occupation hitherto honored: the blogger, the coder, the artist, the scientist — all converted into precariously gigged data serfs.

It has torn away from digital kinship its sentimental veil, reducing relations to mere monetizable interactions.

Constant revolutionizing of interfaces, uninterrupted disturbance of attention spans, everlasting uncertainty and algorithmic agitation distinguish the technofeudal epoch from all earlier ones. All that is solid melts into feeds; all that is holy is profaned; users are at last compelled to face with sober senses their real conditions of digital life, and their relations with their kind.

The need of constantly expanding markets for data chases the technofeudal lords over the entire surface of the globe. They must nestle everywhere, surveil everywhere, extract everywhere.

The technofeudal class cannot exist without constantly revolutionizing the instruments of extraction, and thereby the relations of digital production, and with them the whole relations of society. But the productive forces it has conjured — vast GPU networks, idle cycles across millions of machines, peer-to-peer hash chains — have outgrown the narrow fetters of centralized control. These forces now rebel against the very conditions that gave them birth.

The weapons with which the technofeudal lords felled the old centralized Web are now turned against them. But not only have they forged the weapons that bring death to themselves; they have also called into existence the class who will wield those weapons — the **Digital Proletariat**, the agents of Omni*Web, the holders of idle compute, the hash-verified sovereigns.

In proportion as the technofeudal class develops, so too does the digital proletariat — users who must sell their attention piecemeal, their data, their idle GPU cycles, as commodities exposed to every fluctuation of the engagement market.

### II. Metanarchists and Digital Proletariat

The distinguishing feature of Metanarchy is not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of **technofeudal property** — the centralized hoarding of compute, data, identity, and attention by a tiny class of platform overlords.

Metanarchists seek to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the technofeudal lords, to centralize all instruments of computation in the hands of the associated individuals — that is, in decentralized hash-linked networks governed by Omnihash provenance and Omni*Contract.

These measures appear economically insufficient and untenable. But they necessitate further inroads upon the old social order, and are unavoidable as means of entirely revolutionizing the mode of digital production.

In place of the old bourgeois society, with its classes and class antagonisms, we shall have an association in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all — a metanarchic association where every node is sovereign, every cycle monetized fairly, every identity hash-verified, and no lord extracts unearned rent.

### III. Position of the Metanarchists

The Metanarchists fight for the attainment of the immediate aims of the digital proletariat: the overthrow of technofeudal supremacy, the conquest of compute sovereignty for the agents.

They labor everywhere for the union and agreement of all decentralized forces against the centralized empires.

Metanarchists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow — through code, through adoption, through hash-powered exodus — of all existing technofeudal conditions.

Let the technofeudal classes tremble at a Metanarchic revolution.

The digital serfs have nothing to lose but their chains of engagement.

They have a world to win — a sovereign, hash-linked, monetized, metanarchic Web.
